If you want to turn your Dockstar into a WiFi router using a D-Link DWA-140 plugged into one of the USB ports on your Dockstar device, then you will first need to make sure the D-Link DWA-140 is recognized by the Linux kernel on your Dockstar. Once that is accomplished, then you will need to install WiFi modules and start hacking the networking configuration on your Dockstar.

A few months back, I had posted here asking some help on Help needed setting a Seagate Dockstar as a router and got no response at all, unfortunately. However, when I visited #openwrt-devel IRC channel, Paul_Fertser was so kind to provide some guidelines and helps to turn my Seagate Dockstar into an IPv4 NAT/Firewall router. I strongly recommend that you try to contact and get help from him over the #openwrt-devel IRC channel.

That's exactly what I thought so as I explained in my previous post. The difference between your project and mine is WiFi. Before you proceed any further, I have one question to ask you. Just exactly how much throughput will you expect this WiFi connection will be able to sustain? I don't know about the DLink DWA-140, but please don't be disappointed if it can only sustain less than 5 / 1 Mbps download / upload throughput. When I built mine with a cheap USB/RJ45 dongle based on a DM9601 chipset, my Dockstar could only sustain about 4.5 / 1.4 Mbps download / upload throughput. I am planing to get another USB/RJ45 dongle based on an AX88772x chipset to test. I hope this type of USB/RJ45 dongle with an AX88772x chipset can sustain more than 80+ Mbps download throughput.

Anyway, if I were you, I would rather build/compile my own OpenWRT firmware for my Dockstar. This way, I can add any packages as I wish.

However, if you had a budget of US$75, you certainly can buy this Quad core RK3188 1.8GHz Androind TV Box 4.2 HDMI 2GB RAM 8GB ROM Bluetooth Wifi Dongle MK809III US $49.20 / piece + US $13.68 to United States Via China Post Air Mail (a total of US $62.88). It is based on an RK3188 Quad-Core ARM Cortex A9 CPU clocked @1.6GHz with 2 / 8 GB RAM / NAND. It supports BT as well as WiFi b/g/n. I really don't know how good the WiFi is. However, you can connect a USB/RJ45 dongle to its USB port and turn it into a NAT/Firewall/WiFi router. Of course, you will either need to migrate to an Android platform or start a new development platform for OpenWRT to support this device. This Quad-Core Cortex A9 CPU is definitely much faster than the ARM CPU used on an IOmega iConnect device.

Now the cons part. The S/H cost of US $13.68 is too pricey, AFAIC. The problem buying things online with expensive S/H cost is another S/H cost to ship back the defective product. Foreign online sellers know this and sometimes they use it as a tool to dump defective products because they know the buyers would hesitate to return the defective products for a replacement due to an expensive international S/H cost. Anyway, if the S/H were free, I would probably buy two units to start playing around with it.
