Use the unbrick method.

We have no way of knowing the user accounts and passwords of random firmware images that you download from the Internet. The accounts and passwords that you use in OpenWRT are not retained when you switch over to another firmware.

The 'stripped' firmware images are just TP-Link's own firmware with the bootloader part snipped out. If you want to know the user accounts and passwords of this firmware, ask TP-Link's support for help.

AKAndrew wrote:

The login info for the oirginal firmware should be admin/admin, as displayed on their website. These don't work!

Are you sure you are downloading and using the original firmware from TP-Link's website and stripping it yourself? Or are you per chance downloading a firmware that was stripped by someone else and put available into, say, DropBox or Google Drive? If you are downloading it yourself and it doesn't work, then TP-Link's support is the place where you ask questions. If you are downloading a firmware stripped by someone else, then who knows what other changes was done to it?

Nevertheless, if the credentials they say do not work, then they do not work. End of story, you are locked out. Period, finished, finito. It's the same as forgetting the password of your personal computer.

AKAndrew wrote:

I will eventually be forced to do the unbrick method, but how am I supposed to get back to stock firmware if it won't let me login afterwards?

How am I supposed to know? OpenWRT is a community open-source project and comes with absolutely no warranty. You use it at your own risk. If it doesn't work, or something breaks down, then you and you alone are the person responsible for fixing it. You can questions and discuss problems on this forum, but this question that you're asking is something we cannot possibly know.

You can try one trick that I used once with a completely different router model. Build a ramdisk version of OpenWRT for your router, and run it directly from memory through TFTP boot or through a serial console access to the bootloader. Once it is running, you can mount the root filesystem of the OEM firmware and hack the /etc/passwd and /etc/shadow files to clear the passwords of the OEM firmware. Then you reset the router, boot the OEM firmware and log in with a blank password and change it using 'passwd'.
