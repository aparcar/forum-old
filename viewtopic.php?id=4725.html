In designing your system account for an effective throughput of up to 8 MByte over that kind of distance between two boxes. So that may in practice serve some 6 families but not more.
When operating multiple access points from one spot you can play with directional antennas each covering a particular direction, alternating polarization between adjacent antennas to minimize crosstalk, and note that the channel setting is misleading.
E.g. 802.11 b/g channel 6 is not confined to channel 6 but also radiates inside 2 channels up and down. So effectively you can only operate three APs inside the 12-14 channels when they 'see' each others signals with very high fieldstrenght.
For local distribution of signals we see reliable performance when an access point uses an 8 element collinear vertical antenna and each client an 18 dB 1 meter long yagi antenna. That works smoothly to about 2 km with line of sight with either side reporting 54 Mbit rate in g mode, that is when they don't have something cooking in the microwave.

Fully agree with Vincentfox that it takes much planning of the topology, line of sight considerations, and required capacities. Would also suggest, considering the number of families to use 802.11a (5.47-5.725 GHz) technology as it provides more spectral space, gives more antenna gain for a certain antenna dimension, and is less disturbed by other applications like microwave ovens; but it takes more $$$.  Perhaps a hybrid set-up can do the trick.
Make sure you use boxes and firmware in the long-distance links that can change the timing of 802.11 to adjust for propagation delay; without it the data throughput breaks down. In OpenWrt this distance setting was recently added.

Would suggest to define clusters of clients that all have their own long-distance link (e.g. on basis of 802.11a) from their local access point (802.11g) to the end point of T1.
You may also need a license to operate a telecom company....

I'm just going to post this anyways for informational purposes in case anyone else is thinking of implementing this idea.  I've seen this way to much where wireless hobbyist try to implement something along these lines without any experience in WLANs outside their home or office.

He's some quick down and dirty facts on the feasibility of implementing this scenario.

1.  Surveying:  A professional site survey is needed.  Expect the cost to be in the ballpark of $1000 to $5000.  Outside surveys tend to be in the higher range of cost.  Now it's possible to get a "Surveyor" to do it for less, but those are usually folks who are hobbyists who try to hire out their services.  Most will not provide you the level of detail that you'll want and need (I.E. Detailed topographical maps with signal readings, Spectrum analyzer readings, capacity levels, equipment specs, optimized configurations, Fresnel zone calculations, Inline power requirements, etc).  If you try to skip this step and "do it yourself", you are asking, no, begging for major headaches when and if you actually are up and running.

2.  Going the homebrew route:  While OpenWRT is an awesome package; its purpose is for SOHO (small office/Home Office).  If you really want to become a WISP, you'll need an enterprise class of AP.  Cisco/Airespace, Aruba, Trapeeze, Symbol, Intermec, are but a few vendors in this arena.  Enterprise class APs are more rugged (internally and externally) and offer the flexibility for upgrading and remote management that you'll need unless you plan on spending every waking hour doing on-site trouble calls and constantly tweaking settings manually.  Enterprise class APs range from $200 to $1000.

3.  Who's that knocking on my door?:  Study the FCC regulations (Part 15 and all the clauses).  Something as minor as using an antenna on an AP that is not licensed and approved "as a system" can put your lights out permanently.  $10,000 a day per violation (per AP and antenna) is a costly price to pay because you decided to use a Cisco 10 dBi yagi on a Linksys wireless router.  Each antenna configuration has to be approved by the FCC for use with that specific piece of equipment.  Ignorance of what can work with what can be the death blow to your operation.

4.  Legal stuff:  How and where are you going to mount all those APs? Unless you own all the property, you are going to need some kind of legal agreement with the property owners to place you equipment where you need to.  It costs money to hire a lawyer to draft up and file the required paperwork, licenses, and to represent you in the lawsuit because you skipped step 1 and failed to follow NEC grounding and someone's house burned down or worse, killed them because they were near your improperly grounded equipment.  I know that sounds extreme but it does happen.  The OP mentioned he/she is in Oklahoma.  I know from personal experience that Oklahoma state and local grounding requirements are very strict.  As I'm sure the OP knows, there is ALOT of severe weather that happens there.  Your equipment is going to get taken out eventually.  Just make sure you don't kill one of your customers too.

That is but a few things to consider in implementing a WISP.  I didn't even get into the "3 hop rule" or backup WAN link.
