5gHz frequencies degrade extremely fast when going through concrete and/or metal, and there is nothing that can be done about it due to the physics of the 5gHz wave length.  There are some excellent articles that can be found through google with in depth explanations as to why this occurs with the 5gHz bandwidth and not the 2.4.

Degradation still occurs with the 2.4gHz wavelength through concrete and/or metal; however, while the signal will penetrate further that the 5Ghz wavelength, speed is sacrificed.  You may get better results by increasing power on the 2.4gHz network (possibly playing around with distance settings may help as well, however you'll need to research to figure out what exactly does setting a distance (10m, 20m, etc) do, as I don't know).

Due to the concrete, I would recommend getting a powerline adapter kit (CNET 2016 Review) and a possibly cheap gigabit ethernet 8 port switch (should be no more than ~$20 - 25USD Monoprice has a decent one for $18.99USD).  Powerline adapters utilize the electrical wiring already present in the building to transmit ethernet signalling; they come in pairs, with one plugged into an outlet and connected via ethernet to the switch/router, with the other connected in a different room to an outlet and ethernet going to the device or a second switch/router.

There's a few different ways you could configure this, however I'd recommend placing the WRT1900ac in the room you'll be in most of the time that you'll want wifi access in.  You could then use a cheap 8 port switch in the room where you need network access in, but not necessarily wireless.  As far as ethernet cables go, I recommend buying those as well from MonoPrice due to:

It's unlikely you'll be able to garnish a better 5gHz signal even with increasing power to 30db (1W) if there's a concrete wall in between you and the router.  It's simply physics, as the 5gHz wavelength literally hits a wall and cannot penetrate concrete (electromagnetic radiation literally reflects off concrete [same occurs with ceramic, metal, and obviously mirrors]).  This exact subject was discussed a few months back in the WRT1900AC thread, as another member was also having issues who was also trying to access the 5gHz network through concrete.  (Unfortunately the forum doesn't yet support thread search, as being able to read that conversation would be helpful to you)

Try configuring with 30dB tx power and a Distance Optimization of 50... if that doesn't result with a signal, you're simply not going to get one.  Also, I believe you have to set a specific channel (i.e. not Auto) for tx power to come into play (I could very well be wrong however, as I may be misremembering another conversation in the WRT1900AC thread)
