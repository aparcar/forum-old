It depends on what you want.  I chose the WL-500GP V2 because I needed internal IO (serial ports) and wanted to connect USB devices.  I also wanted to be able to use it as a troubleshooting instrument that is small, lightweight, and cheap.  I will be loading it with a bunch of specific devices: freq counter, logic analyzer, simple scope, RS-485 bus, and some other odds and ends.  I will use it to diagnose and check the health of robots in the field.  Just hook up  web browser from a smart phone, laptop, or computer.

So, maybe you need to determine what you want to do with it, then grab something and make it work.  It doesn't matter until you refine your requirements.  The ultimate does not exist for anything.  It is a feature vs need trade off.  I would rather use the WL-500GP rather than the 3700 because I get 2 serial ports, and 2 usb.  Even though I would like the extra ram and space and use the newer kernel.  So WL-500GP wins on hardware features for my project need.  I hope that helps.

Post #15
lizby
12 Mar 2011, 20:19

There is certainly nothing functionally wrong with a usb-to-serial converter.  I have used a half-dozen or more.  I've had best success with those which are based on the PL2303 chip, but also some success with CH341 (and some failures which I haven't explained).  Ftdi-based converters also work.  Many CH341 dongles are available on ebay for $2-3 US.

It is actually more difficult to use the built-in serial (for which you may have to open the case and solder in wires or a header) for control of external devices, because this is used as the console device, and outputs a great deal of text as the device boots.  You can disable this, but that makes it harder to recover a bricked device.

There are also usb-to-i2c dongles, but I haven't used one.

I think I found a good solution to the lack of serial ports in the router.  They carry a four port $13 USB hub at Walmart.  It is very small and will fit inside the router.  Not as clean as I wanted, but workable.

Edit:
This hub is awesome.  It pops open with no screws, has a very small board inside, and has specs available: http://www.genesyslogic.com/manage/upfi … 255151.pdf .

The cool thing is it the chip may be programmable by the user.  It is a processor in and of itself.  At any rate, it solves my internal IO issues.
