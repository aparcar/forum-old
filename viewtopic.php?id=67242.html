Performing NAT and fire-walling at 1Gbit speeds is going to be challenging for almost all consumer grade routers. Proprietary firmware often gets around this limitation by using some off-load functionality so looks much better in simple benchmarks (say http://www.smallnetbuilder.com/tools/ch â€¦ n-to-lan). But none of these IP/NAT/whatever offloads are integrated into the mainline linux kernel and hence are not available in openwrt/lede (I also heard rumors that the offloads often will only work for a very limited set of firewall rules and are mostly tailored to achieve good numbers for marketing than robust network performance, but that could just be developers declaring the "grapes to sour anyway"). Up to 600Mbps (including traffic shaping), I seem to recall, people reported good results with pcengines' apu2 devices (see http://www.pcengines.ch/apu2.htm). Maybe you should consider turning your existing fritzbox into a dumb AP in one of your rooms while running an APU in the other room (and connect them via ethernet).

Along time ago I strategically went with a router and separate AP to improve coverage.  I happen to have an older PC-Engines ALIX and use it with GB switches with great results.  The router is in the basement (where the cable comes in) and feeds a TP-Link WDR-3600 in an upstairs closet centrally located.  It has some holes in the lower levels, and I have used a HooToo TM-02 bridged wirelessly to fill the gap and my backyard.

You should be able to use an AP and bridge it to a router (with LAN ports) if you align it to the doorways.  This probably will penetrate a closed wood, but not a metal door.  Look for something with removable antennas so you can upgrade if you are not happy with the performance. 

Consider identical devices which support WDS so you can roam between rooms.  You can find some relatively inexpensive travel routers made by GL.inet with removable antennas, but you will only get 300Mbps on 2.4Ghz.  http://www.gl-inet.com/shop/  You can probably get faster with 2 C2600, but at a much higher cost.

Regarding the PC-Engines wireless cards, there is a very limited supply of choices for these and I am not clear if the cards are actually supported in OpenWrt.  Everything needs to be built.  While the cards are dual band cards, they can only be configured to operate on a single band at a time, so for dual band you need 2 cards (hardware restriction, not software).  The PC Engines metal case is only designed for 2 antennas, and one would need to do some drilling to add a second pair for the second card.  As a router, I think they are great, but the reality is that you can get better software that's easier to configure for these (pfSense, IPFire, etc)
