Yes, the CT-14 (and its predecessors, CT-11, CT-12, etc) are really sweet. Good quality headset, dial pad in your pocket, long range from the base unit - it doesn't get much better. I have been using one for over a decade...  But that's not why you wrote :-)

My concern with any VoIP sent over hotel internet is bufferbloat - the undesirable latency induced into the connection from other people's traffic. Most hotels are terrible - I've seen 500msec to 5,000msec (five seconds!) latency, which will play hob with your VoIP... Check out www.dslreports.com/speedtest to measure latency under load.

But if you already own the Magic Jack and the CT-14, then it might be worth the experiment.

Well that other people can call you, your VoIP converter needs to be accessible from the outside, but not hotel network is setup to provide that; most likely they use a NAT box to hide all their guests behind a single IP address or so. So your VoIP box needs to pierce the NAT from the inside so that you are reachable. Most VoIP providers, as far as I know, offer that as a service (since home gateways also come with NAT and fire walling, they need to accommodate for that otherwise they loose non-technical customers); but better check that before buying something...

RangerZ wrote:

I wanted VOIP to take my Home Office number with me, but if the underlying support for "VOIP on the road" is going to leave me with quality issues, I can just continue forwarding to my cell phone and buy a wired headset for it.

There are at least several solutions I am aware of.

  1. If your Home Office phone line is VoIP and its provider also supports multiple registration (with multiple rings), then all you need is just configure your ATA to register to your Home Office phone company to have your incoming calls ring multiple devices.

  2. Some ATA devices have a support for call forwarding using the 2nd line. This option will allow to forward any incoming calls on Line 1 to Line 2 to call out.

  3. If your Home Office line is still a POTs (Plain Old Telephone system), then you will need an ATA with a built-in FXO port to forward any incoming calls to your VoIP line to dial out. An OBi110 is an example of this device.

  4. If all of the above is not possible, then a Digital PBX System @Home Office will come very handy. All you will need is to configure your DIgital PBX System @Home Office with DISA. This requires an additional line.

Perhaps, you may wanna visit DSLR Reports VoIP forum to find out more options on this.

Just one word of caution: OpenWRT ONLY partially supports a PogoPlug Pro, i.e. no WiFi (?). Also, it looks like the current configuration requires OpenWRT to be flashed onto the internal NAND storage. In other words, OpenWRT will not boot off of any external (SATA or USB) storage without a copy of OpenWRT flashed onto the internal NAND storage. WIth a stock firmware still on the internal NAND storage, OpenWRT will boot the Linux kernel and then loads rootfs from the internal storage. This has something to do with the default settings of OpenWRT's Linux kernel configuration, AFAICT.
