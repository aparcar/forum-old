Hello, I am looking for a cheap router with good OpenWRT support and some specific features.

1. Good range: external antenna, 100mW min (the more output power, the better)
2. 11n (150mb min)
3. Wifi chipset well supported under linux (NOT broadcom), I only tried ralink and atheros USB adapters and ralink was working great, not atheros. I dont know if support of integrated wifi chipset is different.
4. USB port, 2.0 min.
5. Easy OpenWRT intallation: firmware upload through HTTP, NOT flashing

I found two routers which meet the requirements: MR3240 ($50), MR3220 ($40), but I wondered if I could find cheaper ones, maybe chinese.

Thanks

I would actually not bother with Chinese routers. TP-Link and most other consumer-range routers are already made in China, their rebrands are usually not that much cheaper, and there's always the possibility that the rebrand switched hardware without anyone knowing.

You're in the right ballpark, I believe. The TP-Link range generally has very good support by OpenWrt and are amongst the most competitively priced. I'd also add the TL-WR842ND to the list, maybe the WR1043ND. (Personally, I'm excited about the Archer C20i, but it is brand new and has no OpenWrt support (yet)).

Did you give second-hand routers a thought? My personal favorite at the moment is the Western Digital MyNet N750, which has been discontinued, and can be had for a fistful of dollars on ebay. (I bought both of mine for ~€11 plus shipping.) Although it does not fit your "external antenna" requirement, they have pretty nice range.

I'd go for a TP-Link 1043. It's cheap and very well supported. Here in germany you can get it for 40€ from Amazon.

If you want it much cheaper, you could try the TP-Link WR842ND, WR841ND or WR841N. The difference beween WR841N and WR841ND is the detachable antenna of the ND version, which makes 6€ difference (18€ for 841N, 25€ for the 841ND). So if you never plan for changing the antenna, the N version is as good as the ND version but 30% cheaper.

I would not expect any AC capable device to reach that low price levels any time soon. The high throughput devices usually come with more memory as well as more powerfull CPUs. I guess those are required to handle a huge amount of concurrent connections. But this leads to more expensiv devices, unfortunately.

If you want 802.11ac, the TP-Link Archer C7 is the best supported by far in openwrt, although even that isn't perfect yet. I'd personally go with 802.11n unless you already have a good need for it.
