As for the OP: Yes, OpenWrt is tougher to get into than other firmware projects. Unlike other firmware projects that come pre-configured and with every bell and whistle included, it pretty much only delivers a baseline function and then it's up to the individual user's needs what to do with it. For people who know what they are doing, or who want to find out and educate themselves this is an enormeous advantage. For those who can't, or outright refuse to, get to know the system this would be its greatest shortcoming.

Yes, you are absolutely right. I would not recommend OpenWrt to anyone who's not able and/or willing to get into the details of what they are doing with it.

Also, yes, OpenWrt has some shortcomings in its, for lack of a better word, "support structure". The wiki is very often very helpful, but not always up-to-date. This is partly remedied by this forum which is basically just one big board for every issue, it could probably be structured a little bit better.

However, what OpenWrt is decidedly not lacking is a very active and very helpful community.

drawz
4 May 2014, 20:03

The WR1043ND v2 is a fairly new router, with a new/different architecture, and with only recent support in trunk only. Remember, trunk is bleeding edge support that is a work in progress. Given all this, it's not surprising that documentation is sparse, not too many people have the device, and there's not much experience with unusual scenarios (e.g. reverting). This is just not the type of router to get if you're first learning or not sure you want to stay on openwrt. I would say the exact same thing about DD-WRT or Tomato. You could also say the same about any piece of software running on any new hardware.

All of that said, the process to revert should be similar to other recent TP-Link routers that do have more documentation (e.g. WDR4300). Give that a shot, using the appropriate files for your router of course, and let us know how it goes. If you figure it out, document it in the wiki. One of the nice things about openwrt compared to other router firmware projects is that it's so much easier to contribute, even if it is as simple as updating the wiki with how to revert.
