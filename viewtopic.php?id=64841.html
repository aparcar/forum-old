A router tends to be running 24/7, in contrast to a workstation running only a couple of hours a day, power consumption does play a big role there. In the past, most x86 systems needed around 130 watts idle. sandy-bridge managed to roundabout half that and ivy-bridge halved that again, a modern haswell or skylake system can go down to 10-15 watts idle (and a router is pretty much idle 98% of the time) and if you select your system properly (Atom range, single voltage boards, etc.) you can easily reach <=6 watts idle without really trying anything special.

Even if you like dumpster diving, for always-on systems you pay dearly through electricity by going with an older desktop-/ workstation (or beware, server class-) system. Easily significantly more than what you'd pay for a brandnew system in a year. When you add wlan to the needs, the costs of two wlan cards, antennas and pigtails also easily cost as much as a complete higher end (compatible) consumer router.

I'm running 18 devices at home, I'm using a NetGear WNR1000 V2 https://wiki.openwrt.org/toh/netgear/wnr1000 which handles 30/30. From the specs you can tell (if your screen reader works with it) that it is not a very powerful device, but it has been very reliable.

It is used as a wifi router/firewall and nothing else. For DNS ad-blocking I use http://alternate-dns.com/ rather than run my own.

I know that you have stated x86 archtecture, but you could consider products from PCEngines http://pcengines.ch/ and they don't cost the earth. They have a nice range of small and powerful server boards, that are used by several product makers, and they are known to work with OpenWRT and PFSense https://www.pfsense.org/. If you find in the future that you need to add more services, then you can get another one. The APU2 seems very capable http://pcengines.ch/apu2b2.htm or http://pcengines.ch/apu2b4.htm. They should be easily available in the UK https://linitx.com/viewcategory.php?cat … amp;pp=176. They are in Suffolk. (screen reader allowing)

I'm sure there are other similar products and someone may give you another recommendation.

I had the same motherboard and it works great with OpenWRT. 8-core is little too powerful so I just run VMWare on it and only use 2 cores on it and still manage to get 700mbit-ish soft-NAT. If I assign 1 more core on it, I can hit the gigabit speed barrier but my internet connection is no where near on it so I just limit it with 2 cores, I can run it with 1 core but well, its better to have those 2 cores just in case.

the rest of the cores runs on my XPnology NAS. Because OpenWRT samba speeds is very very terrible, I really want all this things run on OpenWRT but its just not able to do it.

If youre tight in budget, just buy the other general consumer based boards like a J2900 based SoC motherboards, that would be enough to drive a full gigabit WAN. Then just add a few couple of bucks for Dual or Quad port Intel NIC and youre good to go. Most J2900 based motherboards had a half-height mini pcie port that you can use to insert a compatible wifi card for openwrt. At least in this way it will be a lot cheaper. Power consumption wise, with only the board and a quad nic card and a 8GB flash drive, it only consumes 17watts at full load.
