The 2 antenna go to a single radio, there's no second radio hiding in there. In "auto" or diversity mode, it uses whichever is strongest signal at that moment. If you lock it to a single antenna, it's because you bought a big single antenna and not 2 identical ones.

This should be added to the "Common mistakes" link I think.

The typical way to solve your problem is 2 WiFi units back-to-back. One unit in AP-client mode, ethernet cable connecting to another unit (5 channels distant) broadcasting as an AP.  This is a dual-radio repeater setup, I've done them before and they work well.

It's a bit tricky to be both AP-client, and AP at the same time.

I have run WDS networks before with a single-radio setup. It's somewhat clunky. As traffic rises, performance becomes poor due to the multi-tasking required from a single half-duplex radio.  Particularly timing senstive apps like VoIP becomes choppy.

The small WISP I work with, we use WRT units and anyplace I have a repeater station there are 2 radios.  One unit with directional antenna strictly for backhaul, and a 2nd unit for local client coverage.
