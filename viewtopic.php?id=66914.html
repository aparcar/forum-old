Once you load OpenWrt on these they all have about the same functionality.  The differences are CPU\ram\flash\micro-sd and battery.  Switches mostly do not work as in stock. 

I had an old linksys travel router.  It had the fold out wall connection.  Finding power is not always easy in coffee shops and airports.  5V is much better.  You can connect it to a laptop or an external battery for power..

OpenWrt has no ability to store SSID info (delivered), not sure about other stock firmware.  GLi makes this easy to manage with their GUI. You can hide an SSID in OpenWrt.  Most, but not all devices can find hidden SSIDs.

The InvioBox is the only one that comes with VPN (and the service).  You probably do not want to put OpenWrt on it (even if you can).  Check if the service is supported where you are going.  Your service generally needs to have servers in the country you are traveling in.

For VPN you should look at getting the most horsepower you can get to support encryption, but your gating factor is more likly to be the wireless connection speeds.  Free is not usually fast.  You need VPN to secure your internet traffic on public wifi.

I think antennas are a pain to travel with.  If you break or loose one your in trouble, you can not just run with out them.  You will generally be in line of site in most places other than a hotel room. 

The GLi products are the only ones that come with OpenWrt installed.  All the others have various levels of effort to set them up.  You will need to add packages for USB, mini-DLNA and OpenVPN to meet the above requirements.  The GLi will pretty much have all this.

Looked at the Xiaomi, but it's 12volts.  Means you need AC.

I do not think the AR300M includes DLNA, not the same as file sharing, but not positive.  Easy to fix, so not an issue. 

Pricey?  Your going to spend a year on the road and worried about spending $35 vs $20.  The Kingston is closer to $70.  VPN will cost you $7-10/month.  The delta should be trivial, even to a bunch of poor college students.  Your on the adventure of a lifetime.  You will regret pinching pennies, and I'm not just talking about the router.

Hopefully, the router/repeater would help boost the signal and connection strength

A router will not "boost" a poor signal.  It will help you maintain it (bridge) over distance, though more likely you will be close to the device, as opposed to the device being midway between you and the WISP, so it will really just share the connection.

The "lockout" issue is easy to fix with a script that disables the WWAN on boot or by a button action to do the same.  All OpenWrt devices with a single radio will have this issue.  Dev will not fix the connection issue.  Advantage goes to GLi on this one.  The GLi GUI features include a real easy to use tool that is mobile friendly.  Luci is just to hard to use, and one can easily replace the AP when configuring the STAtion (WISP)

You have the correct link.  I do not have an AR300M, nor do I use this on my AR150, so I can not confirm it works. 

I would change the times in the reset file from 2\5 to 5\15.  This will make it easier to disable the WWAN, but more difficult to reset the device (and wipe the config). 

WDS and relayd should not impact your use case.

What ever OpenWrt device you choose will have the same issue, however the solution relies on a working button, which not all devices have.  You can place this in the startup for those that do not have a button, but this means that you will need to rest the WWAN after every reboot.  May or may not be an issue in your case.
