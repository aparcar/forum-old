Hmm. Standard routers like that, or expandable embedded devices like the Routerboard or Mikrotik equivalents are almost always going to be cheaper than getting your own custom-made stuff, even if you order 10,000 of them. Unless you get them from extreme cheapo chinese manufacturers, at which point you'd be lucky to have them arrive in one piece let alone work properly...

Incidentally, low-end non-dual-radio routers like the TL-WR2543nd or the ever popular 1043nd are extremely cheap, and you can easily make them dual radio by attaching a USB wifi adapter. I find the Atheros ones perform best and you can get a dual-band, dual-stream one for £5 on ebay if you're lucky. Add that to £20-30 for a router (half that if you can stomach second hand) and it's half the price of any "real" dual-band routers.

Well last time I checked the WNDR3700 has long since moved on past the good old v1/v2 days. The v3 is Broadcomm based and not supported, while the v4 is basically a stripped down version of the WNDR4300. The good news is the WNDR4300/3700v4 have almost complete support (it's not quite official yet, but all the major components work) and would fit your bill of technical needs. Yes they are not $30 each, but having gone down the same sort of road in the past as others have pointed out if you plan on using these as a wide-scale long term component of your business you need to plan to pay at least $100/unit.

The more you cut costs on the upfront hardware, the more it will cost you in the long run. Try running putting OpenWRT on 5 TP-Links and running them against the same Openwrt on 5 Netgears or Asus routers. In our experience, the TP-links consistently fail long before the others, simply because the manufacturer cuts corners to make their equipment cheaper. If you're going to be a service provider, you need to look at the total cost of that. Sure you might save say 30-40% upfront on hardware, but if your failure risk/rate is increased by 50-70% is it worth the upfront saving? Since now, knowing the potential failure rate, you have to plan for "replacement stock/inventory" as well as costs of downtime and costs of implementing the replacement policies.

The other major challenge comes from long term viability. Sure some Chinese manufacturer might spec and supply you with the ideal unit... setup your custom build around it, deploy, get all kinds of assurances that they love you forever, etc... but when you use up your initial inventory you go back and ask for more and sure enough they will send you the "better updated version". It will happen, and all of a sudden you're stuck with a deployed business model, customers lining up, and no working solution to deliver. This happens even with the mainstream/retail equipment as manufacturers change product line ups to keep with the times... but the key difference is the mainstream guys give you two things 1) old inventory is usually available for 3-6 months after the product is discontinued/replaced and 2) other open source people (i.e. the openWRT community) tend to care about supporting the new product as it's a common unit that has large appeal. Of course those two points do guarantee anything, but they certainly give you much better odds of being able to develop and transition hardware platforms before you have to start turning customers away.

Anyway your mileage and experiences may vary, but we've been building and selling products based around OpenWRT devices for 8 years now and those are the lessons/conclusions that we've drawn. At the end of the day, the best option is to simply find a good distributor who will give you a bulk/volume discount on the unit you want, and price your product accordingly.

Not one has failed, despite them being run completely out of spec (e.g. outdoors using badly voltage-drooped passive PoE over a damaged/water ingressed ethernet cable in -10'c temperatures and no humidity protection, or stacked on top of each other unventilated such that the chips reach over 80'c). If you are getting lots of failures it's more likely you're doing something wrong.

but the key difference is the mainstream guys give you two things 1) old inventory is usually available for 3-6 months after the product is discontinued/replaced and 2) other open source people (i.e. the openWRT community) tend to care about supporting the new product as it's a common unit that has large appeal. One of the big benefits of Openwrt (particularly since the OP has asked for Atheros devices) is that it makes no difference what the manufacturer does. There's pretty much 100% compatibility between any two devices of the same platform irrespective of manufacturer model or revision, so if they discontinued something you'll find fifty other manufacturers supplying a similar model that will behave exactly the same once booted up.
