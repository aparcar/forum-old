This is a standard forward lookup, nothing to do with reverse lookups. It is nothing to do with DHCP either.

Your router is named Openwrt. Typing its name in the address bar will take it to your router's address.

If you don't want this to happen, change the name to something else.

qasdfdsaq

You can't stop DNS lookups or else the internet would stop working entirely.

The reason you can't type Amazon is because Amazon isn't running on your router. If your router happened to have Amazon's address in its local name zone (or your local zone was .com) then in fact, you *would* be able to just type Amazon.

What you need to do is tell your client computer to stop automatically adding the local ".com" to the address that you type without a dot in them.

alive4ever
9 Jul 2014, 12:51

This is not possible. DNS lookup always occurs, no matter what you are trying to prevent it.
Just change your openwrt hostname to something else, something unfamiliar, something uncommon.

You can experiment with dnsmasq options to achieve your results.

If you are paranoid and for some reason you don't want users of your network to access the openwrt Luci login page, just disable uhttpd service.

on your openwrt console
# /etc/init.d/uhttpd stop
# /etc/init.d/uhttpd disable

So that, all users of your local won't be able to access the OpenWrt Luci login page - even if they type the router ip address.
If you need http configuration enabled, just start uhttpd service and tunnel your traffic to OpenWrt router via ssh tunnel so that your Luci access is secure.

on your local console
$ ssh root@192.168.1.1 -L8000:127.0.0.1:80
on openwrt console
# /etc/init.d/uhttpd start

Then you can access OpenWrt web interface via http://127.0.0.1:8000/ and have your traffic tunneled through ssh tunnel for better security against packet sniffers.

A few points to keep in mind:

a) In any of these scenarios an attacker needs to already have access to your network, maybe even physical access, and the acumen to hack your router's SSH or web interface (even if only through brute force). At this point your router should probably be the least of your worries.
b) It is certainly much harder to randomly try hostnames than to do a coordinated scan through a certainly quite limited range of IP addresses.
c) You usually don't have to look for routers, they are known to the client as the gateway, or they show up in a traceroute.

It may be a convenience issue. But it is certainly not relevant to security. Hacking isn't done through random keypresses, these are not Nintendo consoles of the 1980s.
