Ping is just a measure of speed and loss between 2 points. If you are in say USA and you ping a host in Russia you will get very different result to pinging a server in the same city as you.
The only thing you can change is the connection from your computer to your modem.
So, try pinging the various boxes in your setup. See where in the chain the loss is occuring (my bet would be WiFi - an easy test would be to connect your computer to the TP-link via ethernet).
20-22 is pretty reasonable to a distant host. But losses can be reduced to zero by finding where the losses happen in the chain.

People talk about "ping" but they actually refer to is: Latency.

The latency of any connection between Point A (=your PC) and Point B (=some other PC somewhere) depends on the path the packets take. Packets can and do traverse different paths! It depends on the number of hops in that paths, how quick packets are switched/routed on each individual hop and also, whether the buffers are full or not.

Besides Latency there is Latency variation, or Jitter. Also called ping jump.

To see the number of hops between your PC and the other host try:

traceroute google.jp

You'll see, that your router/routers each represent one hop.
You will also see, that the delay is very low, thus there is nothing to tune.

In case you wanted to upload files (for file sharing) and at the same use VoIP or play an online shooter, you would definitely profit from Configuring your Linux Packet Scheduler
